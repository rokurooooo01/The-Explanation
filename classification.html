1. Domain Terminology: ODE vs. PDE

Before analyzing a differential equation, we must classify its independent variables to determine the mathematical domain we are working within.

  • Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE): Contains derivatives with respect to a single independent variable. This course focuses entirely on ODEs. Example: $\frac{dy}{dx} + 3y = e^x$.
  • Partial Differential Equation (PDE): Contains partial derivatives with respect to multiple independent variables. Example: $\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = 0$.

2. System Hierarchy: Order & Degree

Every differential equation has a hierarchical structure defined by its derivatives.

  • Order: The strictly highest derivative operation present in the equation.
  • Degree: The algebraic power (exponent) of the highest-order derivative, provided the equation is free of fractional powers or radicals.
Proofing the Degree (Clearing Radicals Trap):
Determine the order and degree of the following equation:
$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^3}$$ Step 1: Justification of State. You cannot determine the degree while derivatives are trapped inside a radical (fractional exponent). You must prove its true polynomial form first.

Step 2: Algebraic Manipulation. Square both sides to eliminate the square root:
$$\left(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right)^2 = 1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^3$$ Conclusion: The highest derivative is $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$, so the Order is 2. Looking at that specific term, its exponent is 2, so the Degree is 2. *(Note: We ignore the degree 3 on the first derivative!)*

3. Constraints of Linearity

An ODE of order $n$ is classified as Linear if it perfectly matches the standard structural format:

$$a_n(x)\frac{d^ny}{dx^n} + a_{n-1}(x)\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}} + \dots + a_1(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + a_0(x)y = g(x)$$

To quickly prove if an equation is Non-Linear, look for any of these three rule violations:

  1. Power Violation: The dependent variable $y$ or any of its derivatives have a degree greater than 1 (e.g., $y^2$ or $\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^3$).
  2. Product Violation: The dependent variable $y$ is multiplied by its own derivative (e.g., $y\frac{dy}{dx}$).
  3. Transcendental Violation: The dependent variable $y$ is trapped inside a non-linear function (e.g., $\sin(y)$, $e^y$, or $\ln(y)$).
Linearity Diagnostics Examples:
  • $x^2\frac{dy}{dx} + y = x^3$ $\implies$ Linear. (The $x^2$ is fine, linearity only restricts $y$).
  • $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ $\implies$ Non-Linear. (Violates Rule 2: $y$ is multiplied by its derivative).
  • $\frac{dy}{dx} + \cos(y) = 0$ $\implies$ Non-Linear. (Violates Rule 3: $y$ is inside a trigonometric function).
