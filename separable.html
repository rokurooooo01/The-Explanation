1. The Architecture of Separability

A first-order differential equation is considered Separable if its terms can be factored perfectly into a product of a pure $x$-function and a pure $y$-function.

Standard Factorization Profile: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)h(y)$$

If the equation contains terms like $(x + y)$ or $\sin(xy)$ that cannot be algebraically separated by multiplication or division, it fails the separability test and requires more advanced methods (like those in Chapters 3 or 5).

2. Standard Operational Strategy

Once you verify the equation can be factored, execute the following protocol:

  1. Isolate Variables: Shift all $y$ elements (including $dy$) to the left side, and all $x$ elements (including $dx$) to the right side.
  2. Direct Integration: Apply the integral operator to both sides independently.
  3. General Solution Form: Combine the arbitrary integration constants from both sides into a single master constant $+ C$ (typically placed on the independent $x$-side).
  4. Explicit Isolation (If Possible): Solve algebraically for $y$ to yield $y = f(x)$. If the algebra is impossible, leave it as an implicit solution.
Proofing Example: The General Solution
Solve the differential equation: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2x}{3y^2}$

Step 1: Isolate via cross-multiplication.
$$3y^2 dy = 2x dx$$ Step 2 & 3: Integrate both sides and add the constant $C$.
$$\int 3y^2 dy = \int 2x dx \implies y^3 = x^2 + C$$ Step 4: Solve explicitly for $y$.
$$y = \sqrt[3]{x^2 + C}$$

3. Initial Value Problems (IVP)

Often, engineers require a Particular Solution rather than a General Solution. If given an initial coordinate point (an initial condition like $y(0) = 5$), you must plug these values into the General Solution to solve for the exact value of $C$.

IVP Calculation Matrix:
Find the particular solution to $\frac{dy}{dx} = -xy$ subject to the initial condition $y(0) = 4$.

1. Separate and Integrate:
$$\frac{1}{y} dy = -x dx \implies \int \frac{1}{y} dy = \int -x dx$$ $$\ln|y| = -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + C$$ 2. Exponentiate to isolate $y$:
$$y = e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2 + C} = e^C \cdot e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}$$ (Since $e^C$ is just another constant, we rename it $K$)
$$y = K e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}$$ 3. Apply Initial Condition $y(0) = 4$:
$$4 = K e^{-\frac{1}{2}(0)^2} \implies 4 = K(1) \implies K = 4$$ Particular Solution: $$y = 4e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}$$
