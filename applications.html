1. Physics: Kinematics & Free Fall

Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$) is fundamentally a differential equation, because acceleration $a$ is the derivative of velocity $v$ with respect to time $t$ ($a = \frac{dv}{dt}$).

If an object is falling through the atmosphere, it experiences gravity pulling it down and air resistance pushing back up (proportional to its velocity). The equation governing its speed is a Linear First-Order Equation:

$$m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - kv$$

Where $m$ is mass, $g$ is gravity, and $k$ is the aerodynamic drag coefficient.

2. Biology: Population Dynamics

If a population has unlimited resources, it grows exponentially. However, environments have a maximum capacity they can sustain, known as the carrying capacity ($K$).

The Logistic Growth Model is a non-linear differential equation (specifically, a Bernoulli Equation) that models how a population $P$ grows quickly at first, but slows down as it approaches the limit $K$:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)$$

Where $r$ is the natural growth rate. Notice that as $P$ gets closer to $K$, the term in parentheses approaches zero.

3. Electrical Engineering: Circuit Analysis

Differential equations dictate how electricity flows through components over time. In a simple RL (Resistor-Inductor) circuit, Kirchhoff's Voltage Law states that the sum of voltage drops must equal the voltage supplied by the battery $V(t)$.

The current $i$ in the circuit is governed by a Linear First-Order Equation:

$$L\frac{di}{dt} + Ri = V(t)$$

Where $L$ is inductance and $R$ is resistance. This is usually solved using the Integrating Factor method.

4. Thermodynamics: Newton's Law of Cooling

Whether it is a cup of coffee cooling in a room or a forged piece of steel quenching in an oil bath, the rate at which an object changes temperature is directly proportional to the difference between its own temperature ($T$) and the ambient environment temperature ($T_m$).

This creates a highly reliable Separable Differential Equation:

$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_m)$$

Where $k$ is the thermal conductivity constant of the object. Integrating this yields a logarithmic decay curve.

5. Chemistry & Fluid Systems: Mixture Problems

A classic engineering problem involves a tank containing a volume of fluid. A chemical solution is pumped in at a specific rate and concentration, mixes instantly, and drains out at another rate. We use a Linear Equation to track the exact mass of the chemical $A(t)$ remaining in the tank.

$$\frac{dA}{dt} = (\text{Rate In}) - (\text{Rate Out})$$ $$\frac{dA}{dt} = c_{in}r_{in} - \left(\frac{A}{V(t)}\right)r_{out}$$

Where $c_{in}$ is the incoming concentration, $r$ represents flow rates, and $V(t)$ is the dynamic volume of the tank.

6. Economics: Continuous Compound Interest

If a bank compounds your interest annually, monthly, or daily, you use standard algebra. But what if the interest is compounding continuously—every single fraction of a microsecond?

The rate of change of your account balance ($B$) becomes strictly proportional to the amount currently in the account. This yields the simplest Separable Equation of them all:

$$\frac{dB}{dt} = rB$$

Where $r$ is the continuous interest rate. Solving this by separating variables directly results in the famous PERT equation: $B(t) = B_0 e^{rt}$.
